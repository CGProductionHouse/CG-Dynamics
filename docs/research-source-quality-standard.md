# CG Dynamics Research and Source Quality Standard

## Purpose

CG Dynamics must not manufacture expertise by turning plausible language into Skill Cards, industry guidance, platform knowledge or AI instructions.

Every reusable knowledge item must be traceable to legitimate evidence, reviewed for fit and limitations, and labelled honestly according to what the source can actually support.

This standard applies to:

- Skill Cards;
- universal marketing principles;
- discipline knowledge;
- industry packs;
- platform knowledge;
- AI specialist instructions;
- campaign recommendations presented as reusable guidance;
- operating procedures represented as best practice;
- benchmarks, claims and performance guidance.

## Non-negotiable rule

No reusable skill, rule, framework or recommendation may be activated merely because it sounds sensible.

A knowledge item may only become operational when:

1. the source is identifiable;
2. the source is credible for the type of claim being made;
3. the relevant passage, data or evidence has actually been reviewed;
4. the wording does not overstate what the source proves;
5. limitations, context and known exceptions are recorded;
6. date-sensitive claims are still current;
7. a human reviewer has approved the resulting item;
8. the item passes the existing activation gate.

## Source hierarchy

### Grade A — Primary and authoritative

Preferred for factual, technical, legal, scientific and platform claims.

Examples:

- official platform documentation;
- legislation, regulation and regulator guidance;
- original peer-reviewed research;
- recognised professional standards;
- original datasets and first-party measurement;
- approved client-provided facts;
- original books or works by the person whose method is being represented.

Use Grade A sources whenever available.

### Grade B — Strong expert synthesis

Useful when the author has demonstrable expertise and accurately synthesises stronger underlying evidence.

Examples:

- respected academic or professional textbooks;
- systematic reviews and meta-analyses;
- recognised industry bodies;
- established expert books with enduring professional adoption;
- high-quality research organisations that disclose methods and sources.

Grade B sources may support practical guidance but must not silently replace a more current primary source for changing platform rules, legislation or specifications.

### Grade C — Credible practitioner evidence

Useful for tested workflows, operational patterns and informed hypotheses.

Examples:

- experienced practitioners with transparent case evidence;
- reputable agency or consultancy research with a disclosed method;
- documented internal CG results;
- high-quality case studies with clear context and limitations.

Grade C sources must be labelled as practitioner evidence, observed pattern or measured internal result. They may not be presented as universal law.

### Grade D — Discovery only

May help identify a topic but cannot independently support an active Skill Card.

Examples:

- unsourced blog posts;
- social posts and short-form videos;
- anonymous articles;
- generic AI-generated summaries;
- affiliate content;
- listicles;
- isolated testimonials;
- opinion pieces without evidence;
- popularity or star ratings without subject-matter validation.

Grade D material may point researchers toward stronger sources. It must not become approved organisational knowledge on its own.

## Reputation and review signals

Popularity, sales rank, ratings and reviews are useful screening signals, not proof of accuracy.

For books and expert sources, reviewers should consider:

- the author's qualifications and direct experience;
- whether the work is recognised within the relevant profession;
- whether later scholarship or practice supports, refines or contradicts it;
- the publisher or institution;
- the quality and number of independent reviews;
- whether positive reviews come from credible practitioners or scholars;
- whether the source clearly distinguishes evidence from opinion;
- whether the work remains applicable to the current problem.

A famous or highly rated book may still be outdated, context-limited or weak evidence for a specific claim.

## Claim-to-source matching

The source must be appropriate for the claim.

Examples:

- current Instagram specifications require current official Meta documentation;
- a behavioural claim should rely on credible behavioural research, not only a copywriting book;
- a legal or HR rule requires current legislation or qualified professional guidance;
- a client fact requires approved client or verified internal information;
- a performance benchmark requires a disclosed dataset and comparable context;
- an internal workflow may rely on verified CG operating knowledge;
- a creative recommendation may use expert practice but must be framed as guidance, not fact.

No source may be stretched beyond its actual subject, geography, population, date range or methodology.

## Minimum evidence record

Every proposed reusable knowledge item should retain:

- title of the source;
- author or issuing organisation;
- publication or effective date;
- source grade;
- source type;
- URL, ISBN, DOI or stable identifier where applicable;
- exact chapter, section, page, table or passage where practical;
- concise description of the evidence reviewed;
- claim supported;
- limitations and exceptions;
- geography or audience limits;
- last verified date;
- review or expiry date;
- reviewer notes;
- confidence level;
- evidence label: documented fact, measured result, expert interpretation, observed pattern, hypothesis or preference.

A citation to an entire book without identifying the relevant basis is insufficient for a precise operational claim.

## Corroboration rules

One source is enough only when it is the definitive primary authority for the claim, such as an official product specification or approved client fact.

Corroboration is normally required when:

- the claim is contested;
- the source is secondary or practitioner-led;
- the recommendation may materially affect spend, reputation or client results;
- the evidence is old;
- the claim is broad or universal;
- the source has a commercial interest;
- different reputable sources disagree.

Where reliable sources disagree, the Skill Card must record the disagreement and context. The reviewer may narrow the claim, lower confidence, require testing or reject activation.

## Books and foundational works

Books are valuable for durable principles, expert methods, historical context and coherent professional frameworks.

They must not be used as proof of:

- current platform algorithms;
- current advertising policies;
- current legal requirements;
- current technical limits;
- guaranteed performance;
- universal human behaviour without supporting evidence.

When converting a book into Skill Cards:

1. use the original work rather than a third-party summary where possible;
2. identify the relevant chapter or passage;
3. separate the author's principle from the author's examples;
4. check whether credible modern evidence supports or limits the principle;
5. record where the method is opinion, professional judgement or measured evidence;
6. avoid converting every chapter into a rule merely to increase card count.

## Platform knowledge

Platform knowledge is date-sensitive by default.

The preferred evidence order is:

1. current official platform documentation;
2. official announcements or product release notes;
3. official support or policy pages;
4. documented first-party CG testing;
5. strong independent testing with disclosed methods;
6. practitioner observation labelled as provisional.

Platform rumours, social-media commentary and unverified algorithm claims must not be activated as facts.

## Internal and client knowledge

Internal knowledge is legitimate only when ownership and approval are clear.

Examples include:

- approved CG policies and procedures;
- verified workflow definitions;
- measured CG performance;
- approved client brand guidance;
- client-confirmed facts and constraints;
- recorded decisions from authorised people.

A chat message, meeting note or staff opinion does not automatically become durable truth. It must be attributed, scoped and approved.

## Research workflow

For each proposed knowledge area:

1. define the exact question or claim;
2. identify the strongest appropriate source class;
3. retrieve and review the actual source material;
4. capture the evidence record;
5. seek corroboration where required;
6. identify contradictions and limitations;
7. draft the smallest accurate Skill Card or knowledge item;
8. label confidence and evidence type;
9. submit for substantive human review;
10. activate only after the governance gate passes;
11. schedule review or expiry where needed;
12. monitor usage and measured outcomes without retroactively overstating the evidence.

## Prohibited practices

CG Dynamics must not:

- create skills from model memory alone;
- cite sources that were not actually checked;
- invent page numbers, quotations, studies or statistics;
- treat ratings, popularity or bestseller status as proof;
- turn one person's opinion into a universal rule;
- use a secondary summary when a necessary primary source is available and practical to review;
- conceal contradictory evidence;
- present correlation as causation;
- present a case study as guaranteed performance;
- copy current platform advice from an outdated book;
- activate placeholder or thinly researched cards to make the library look populated;
- use former-client information as reusable industry knowledge;
- allow an AI specialist to make unsupported client-facing claims.

## Review test

Before approving any item, the reviewer must be able to answer:

- What exact claim are we storing?
- What source supports it?
- Did we inspect the actual source?
- Is that source appropriate for this claim?
- What does it not prove?
- Is it current enough?
- Is corroboration required?
- Is the wording narrower than or equal to the evidence?
- Is the confidence label honest?
- Would we be comfortable showing the source and reasoning to a client or qualified expert?

If any material answer is unclear, the item remains `needs_review`.

## Product requirement

Future knowledge creation interfaces should make weak sourcing difficult by requiring structured evidence fields, source grading, limitations, review notes and freshness data before activation.

The goal is not the largest knowledge library. The goal is a smaller, defensible and continuously improving body of knowledge that CG staff and clients can trust.
