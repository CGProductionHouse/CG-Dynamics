# Golden Marketing Source Pack 01

**Status:** Active research pack  
**Branch:** `feature/ai-workforce-db-design`  
**Purpose:** Build a human, culturally aware, commercially sharp marketing agent from real human work rather than generic AI-generated patterns.

## Core objective

The future CG Dynamics marketing agent should think less like a text generator and more like a strong head of marketing or creative director.

The standard is work that is:

- Specific.
- Culturally aware.
- Commercially useful.
- Human enough to sound spoken, not manufactured.
- Distinctive enough to belong to one brand.
- Simple enough to remember.
- Brave enough to take a point of view.
- Relevant enough to deserve attention.

The aim is not to make all work old-fashioned. The aim is to recover the discipline, wit, observation and care that strong advertising used before generic AI language became common.

## Source handling rule

Do not upload or reproduce copyrighted books or campaign assets in full.

For copyrighted works, store only:

- Bibliographic metadata.
- Source notes.
- Verified principles.
- Short compliant quotations where necessary.
- Page, chapter or section references from a lawfully accessed copy.
- Distinct Skill Cards derived from verified reading.

Public-domain material may be examined more deeply, but the library should still store principles and notes rather than unnecessary full-text duplication.

## Modern pre-generative-AI campaign sources

The main modern reference window should prioritise work from approximately 1990–2022, with earlier classics used where helpful.

This avoids treating the last three years of increasingly AI-shaped brand language as the default standard.

### Coca-Cola official campaign archive

Primary source: The Coca-Cola Company history and media archive  
URL: https://www.coca-colacompany.com/

Priority cases:

#### Share a Coke

Official case background: https://www.coca-colacompany.com/about-us/history/how-a-campaign-got-its-start-down-under

Why it matters:

- A simple product-level idea changed the packaging itself.
- Personal names created participation and emotional ownership.
- The idea encouraged real social behaviour rather than merely describing connection.
- The creative proposition was easy to explain and difficult to ignore.

Principle candidate:

> Strong campaigns often create a behaviour people can participate in, not merely a message they can read.

#### Boys on a Bench

Official background: https://www.coca-colacompany.com/about-us/history/coca-colas-original-work-that-matters

Why it matters:

- Cultural context gave a simple image deeper meaning.
- The brand took a visible point of view without cluttering the idea.
- Human truth and symbolism carried the campaign.

Principle candidate:

> Cultural relevance comes from understanding the meaning already present in a moment, not adding fashionable language afterward.

#### Coca-Cola design continuity

Official background: https://www.coca-colacompany.com/about-us/history/coca-cola-red-our-second-secret-formula

Why it matters:

- Strong brands reuse and reinterpret distinctive assets.
- The past is not copied literally; it becomes a foundation.
- Recognition compounds when visual and verbal cues remain coherent.

Principle candidate:

> Distinctive brand assets should evolve without being discarded every time the campaign changes.

### Nando's South Africa

Primary source: Official Nando's South Africa brand material  
URL: https://www.nandos.co.za/explore/brand-story

Why it matters:

- Brand language comes from a recognisable point of view.
- Southern African sign-writing and visual culture inform the identity.
- The tone is playful, local and confident.
- The brand is willing to comment on culture and current events.
- The humour works because it belongs to Nando's, not because it is generically “fun”.

Research themes:

- Local cultural observation.
- Timing.
- Short-form topical copy.
- Double meaning.
- Confidence without corporate filler.
- Distinctive brand devices.
- Humour that reinforces the brand rather than distracting from it.

Important warning:

Do not reduce the lesson to “make jokes like Nando's”. The transferable skill is not imitation. It is disciplined observation, relevance, timing and a clear brand point of view.

## First extraction programme

### Batch A — direct-response discipline

Sources:

- Scientific Advertising.
- My Life in Advertising.

Target Skill Cards:

- Sell the real thing, not an adjective.
- Give a reason to believe.
- Let the headline select the right audience.
- Specific claims outperform empty praise.
- Build from observation before writing.

### Batch B — human and culturally aware brand work

Sources:

- Share a Coke.
- Boys on a Bench.
- Nando's South Africa brand material.
- Selected pre-2023 award-winning campaigns.

Target Skill Cards:

- Create participation, not just messaging.
- Find the human behaviour already happening.
- Use culture as context, not decoration.
- Build a recognisable brand point of view.
- Use humour only when it belongs to the brand.
- Say less when the idea is strong.

### Batch C — headline and poster language

Sources:

- Duke Ad*Access.
- Historic newspapers.
- Historic poster collections.
- Pre-2023 social posts from verified brand accounts.

Target Skill Cards:

- Headline before filler.
- One poster, one dominant message.
- Use nouns and verbs before adjectives.
- Make the subject visible in the wording.
- Let the offer or tension carry the copy.
- Prefer a concrete line people can repeat.

## Human-copy evaluation dataset

Build a review set containing:

- 100 historic print advertisements.
- 50 pre-2023 social posts from distinctive brands.
- 30 South African campaigns.
- 20 strong topical ads.
- 20 weak generic AI-style examples.
- 20 false positives: polished human copy that may look simple but is not generic.

Each item should be labelled for:

- Specificity.
- Speakability.
- Brand distinctiveness.
- Cultural relevance.
- Commercial clarity.
- Emotional truth.
- Memorability.
- Generic-language risk.
- Evidence or reason to believe.
- Whether the line could belong to an unrelated brand.

## Current conclusion

The future agent should not be trained to imitate one famous brand.

It should learn the deeper habits shared by strong human marketers:

1. Notice something true.
2. Choose a point of view.
3. Connect it to the product or business goal.
4. Express it simply.
5. Remove every word that does not earn attention.
6. Preserve the client's own voice.
7. Test whether the work could belong to anyone else.

The output standard is not “sounds creative”.

The output standard is:

> A person clearly noticed something, made a decision and cared enough to say it properly.
