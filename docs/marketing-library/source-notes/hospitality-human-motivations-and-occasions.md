# Source Note: Hospitality Human Motivations and Occasions

Last updated: 2026-07-20
Status: Draft source synthesis; suitable for Skill Card review, not automatic activation
Domain: Hospitality marketing

## Research question

What are people actually buying when they choose a restaurant, bar, coffee shop, takeaway or food-led experience, and how should that change marketing?

## Source set

### Claude Hopkins — Scientific Advertising

Type: Public-domain book
Authority: Foundational advertising practitioner text
Primary source: https://www.loc.gov/item/23009362/

Relevant principles:
- Advertising should function as salesmanship.
- Specific claims are more persuasive than broad superlatives.
- Individuality matters.
- Advertising decisions should be tested rather than defended as taste.

Modern translation:
Hospitality marketing should make a concrete proposition connected to a real customer situation. A beautiful image and generic praise are not enough.

Risk:
Hopkins wrote in a different era. Platform tactics and modern examples must be labelled as translations, not attributed to the original text.

### South African Department of Tourism — Gastronomy Tourism Framework

Type: Official government framework
Primary source: https://www.tourism.gov.za/CurrentProjects/Pages/Gastronomy_Tourism_Framework.aspx

Relevant findings:
- Food experiences connect visitors with culture, heritage and traditions.
- Gastronomy can support social cohesion and local economic opportunity.
- Destinations should identify local culinary products and develop marketing around them.

Marketing implication:
Food is not only a product category. It can carry identity, memory, place and participation. Hospitality marketing should identify the local story that competitors cannot truthfully copy.

### South African Tourism — Gastronomy and national identity

Type: Official tourism organisation editorial source
Primary source: https://www.southafrica.net/gl/en/corporate/press/south-african-gastronomy-where-local-ingredients-tell-the-story-of-a-nation

Relevant findings:
- South African food is presented as memory, heritage, resilience and identity.
- Regional ingredients and food traditions provide distinct stories.
- Homogenous global experiences increase the value of locally specific food experiences.

Marketing implication:
Do not sand away regional, linguistic or cultural specificity in an attempt to sound universally polished.

### Statistics South Africa — Food and Beverages survey

Type: Official recurring statistical survey
Primary source: https://isibaloweb.statssa.gov.za/pages/surveys/ets/monthly/Food%20%26%20Beverages/food.php

Relevant scope:
Stats SA tracks restaurants and coffee shops, takeaway and fast-food outlets, and catering services through a recurring survey of VAT-registered enterprises.

Marketing implication:
The hospitality domain should not be treated as one uniform business type. Subcategory, service model and customer occasion affect the strategy.

### National Restaurant Association — research reports

Type: Industry association research
Primary source: https://restaurant.org/research-and-media/research/research-reports/

Useful recurring findings:
- Value and convenience influence restaurant decisions.
- Dining remains a meaningful discretionary priority even under pressure.
- Word of mouth and digital discovery both matter.
- Local food and memorable experiences are important to travellers.
- Off-premises dining is a major behavioural category.

Geographic limitation:
The association is US-based. Findings should be treated as useful behavioural evidence, not automatically transferred to South Africa without local validation.

### South African Government — Public holidays and school calendar

Primary sources:
- https://www.gov.za/about-sa/public-holidays
- https://www.gov.za/about-sa/school-calendar

Marketing implication:
Hospitality demand is occasion-sensitive. Public holidays, school terms and family routines create planning triggers, but dates must be verified annually.

## Synthesised principles

### 1. Hospitality demand is occasion-led

The same person may choose entirely different venues and products depending on whether they are:
- late for work
- meeting a client
- taking children out
- celebrating
- watching sport
- travelling
- exhausted after work
- seeking novelty

Therefore, a useful audience definition often combines person + occasion + state of mind.

### 2. Hospitality offers multiple kinds of value

Value may mean:
- low price
- large portion
- convenience
- speed
- confidence that a group will be happy
- an experience worth leaving home for
- local authenticity
- social status
- emotional comfort

The expert must not reduce value to discounting.

### 3. Specificity creates appetite and credibility

Concrete sensory and product detail is more persuasive than generic adjectives.

Weak:
"An irresistible dining experience."

Stronger direction:
Name the dish, preparation, texture, portion, occasion or proof that makes it desirable.

### 4. Place and culture are commercial assets

A local dish, ritual, language choice, supplier, regular customer or community pattern can create distinction that generic brand language removes.

### 5. Participation improves cultural relevance

People often value experiences they can join, discuss, choose or share. Hospitality naturally supports participation through tables, crowds, choices, rituals, sport and events.

### 6. Timing is part of the proposition

A lunch post after lunch, a booking push after the venue is full, or a cold-drink post during cold weather may be strategically weak even when the content itself is good.

The expert should connect publishing time to likely decision time.

## Candidate Skill Cards

- Market the occasion, not only the product
- Define value before choosing a promotion
- Use sensory specificity instead of praise
- Connect food to place, culture or memory when true
- Match publishing time to customer decision time
- Treat dine-in and off-premises as distinct behaviours
- Use people and participation to sell atmosphere

## Verification flags

- South African customer interviews and client performance data are still required.
- US restaurant research must not be presented as South African fact.
- Cultural examples require local review.
- Current consumer trends need review dates.
- Calendar dates must expire annually.
