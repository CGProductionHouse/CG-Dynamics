# Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die

**Authors:** Chip Heath and Dan Heath  
**Year published:** 2007  
**Source type:** Foundational book / Communication psychology  
**Tier:** 1 — Primary and foundational

## Why this source matters

Made to Stick explains why some ideas are remembered and acted on while others are ignored. The authors introduce the SUCCESs framework: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories.

This is directly useful for advertising copy, headlines, campaign concepts, client presentations and internal briefs. It bridges psychology and practical communication.

## What it can support

- How to make messages memorable.
- The SUCCESs framework and each component.
- Why concrete details outperform abstract claims.
- How to use unexpected openings to capture attention.
- Why stories are more persuasive than data alone.
- How to test whether a message is sticky before publishing.
- Writing headlines, hooks and core messages that survive in a crowded feed.

## What it cannot support

- Visual design or video production principles.
- Brand positioning or long-term brand strategy.
- Platform specific advertising tactics.
- South African consumer behaviour data.
- Quantitative campaign measurement or attribution.
- SEO, keyword research or search behaviour.
- Paid ads bidding, audience targeting or creative testing frameworks.
- Client reporting structure or data presentation beyond the principle of making numbers concrete.

## Future Skill Card candidates

- The SUCCESs framework overview.
- Simple: finding the core of the message.
- Unexpected: breaking patterns to grab attention.
- Concrete: making ideas tangible.
- Credible: using authentic authority.
- Emotional: making people care.
- Stories: the mental flight simulator.
- The curse of knowledge and how to avoid it.

## Notes to verify manually

- Confirm that the SUCCESs acronym is used as a teaching framework, not a rigid formula. Some later readers treat it as a checklist, which was not the authors' intention.
- Check whether specific examples in the book translate well to South African and small business advertising context. Some examples are large American campaigns.
- Verify the research studies cited for each principle. The book is well sourced but some studies may have context that matters for accurate application.
- Note that sticky does not always mean true or ethical. A misleading message can also be sticky. Skill Cards should acknowledge this distinction.
- The curse of knowledge concept is widely useful and should be one of the first cards extracted.
