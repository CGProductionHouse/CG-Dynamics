# Scientific Advertising

**Author:** Claude Hopkins  
**Year published:** 1923 (reprinted 1966)  
**Source type:** Foundational book  
**Tier:** 1 — Primary and foundational

## Why this source matters

Scientific Advertising is widely considered the first book to treat advertising as a measurable, results-driven discipline rather than a guessing game. Hopkins introduced the concept of split testing, coupon tracking, and the idea that advertising should be built on measurable returns, not awards.

It remains relevant because the principles of direct response, measurable offers, and customer psychology have not changed, even if the media has.

## What it can support

- The case for measurement in advertising.
- Principles of direct response: offers, headlines, calls to action.
- The concept of serving the customer rather than showing off.
- The importance of tracking and split testing.
- The idea that advertising is salesmanship in print (or screen).
- Why generic claims and superlatives weaken trust.
- Why price alone is rarely the best argument.

## What it cannot support

- Modern algorithmic advertising mechanics (Meta, Google Ads bidding, audience targeting).
- Brand building as a separate long-term strategy (Hopkins was pure direct response).
- Social media content strategy, influencer marketing or engagement metrics.
- Visual design, video production or creative direction outside direct response.
- Multi-channel attribution models.
- South African market specific behaviour.
- Any claim about modern platform algorithms or ranking signals.

## Future Skill Card candidates

- Advertising is salesmanship.
- The value of split testing.
- Offers over superlatives.
- Specificity over general claims.
- Tracking before scaling.
- Serve the customer, not the award.
