# Source Note: Historical Advertising and Human Copy

**Status:** Research foundation complete; extraction in progress  
**Knowledge layer:** Universal marketing / copywriting / creative direction  
**Purpose:** Build a verified evidence base for writing that sounds human, specific and commercially useful rather than generic AI copy.

## Why this source group matters

Modern AI-generated marketing frequently defaults to vague benefit language, empty transformation promises and polished corporate phrasing. Examples include:

- Enhance your look.
- Elevate your experience.
- Unlock your potential.
- Discover the difference.
- Where quality meets excellence.
- Designed with you in mind.

These phrases are grammatically acceptable but commercially weak because they usually omit the real product, situation, audience, tension, proof, offer or action.

Historical advertising is useful because older ads had to earn attention in expensive print space. Strong examples commonly used direct headlines, concrete product facts, offers, demonstrations, reasons to believe, urgency, curiosity, local language and recognisable human situations.

This source group is not used to imitate outdated language or social values. It is used to recover disciplined communication principles.

## Primary sources

### Claude C. Hopkins — Scientific Advertising, 1923

- Source type: Public-domain book.
- Primary location: Library of Congress digitised book.
- Relevant chapters include Just Salesmanship, Headlines, Being Specific, Individuality, Offer Service and Test Campaigns.
- Supported use: Direct-response principles, specificity, headline relevance, service to the buyer, individuality and measurable claims.
- Limitation: Period-specific examples must be translated carefully. Modern platform mechanics cannot be attributed to Hopkins.

### Library of Congress — Chronicling America and historic advertising collections

- Source type: Primary-source newspaper and poster archives.
- Scope: Historic newspapers, advertisements and posters across multiple decades.
- Supported use: Study how headlines, offers and public communication were structured before generative AI.
- Research value: Full-page context reveals hierarchy, headline length, product naming, proof, price, urgency, local relevance and call-to-action patterns.
- Limitation: Historical material may include stereotypes, unsafe claims or outdated social assumptions. Extract communication structure, not harmful content.

### Duke University — Ad*Access

- Source type: Primary-source archive from the J. Walter Thompson Company Archives.
- Scope: More than 7,000 magazine and newspaper advertisements, mainly 1911–1955.
- Supported use: Comparative study of headlines, body copy, product demonstrations, consumer tensions, proof and visual hierarchy.
- Copyright note: Use for research, teaching and private study with attribution. Do not republish copyrighted advertisements as production assets.

### Library of Congress — WPA, World War I and advertising poster collections

- Source type: Primary-source poster archives.
- Supported use: Study short-form persuasive communication, visual hierarchy, imperative headlines, public calls to action and economy of language.
- Limitation: Propaganda and public-information goals differ from commercial advertising. Principles must be translated, not copied blindly.

## Early patterns confirmed from the source group

1. **Name the real thing.** Strong advertising usually identifies the product, event, offer, problem or audience instead of hiding behind abstract benefit language.
2. **Use concrete details.** Price, place, quantity, timing, ingredients, method, proof and circumstance make copy believable.
3. **Write for a situation.** Human copy recognises what the reader is doing, feeling, needing or deciding.
4. **Lead with relevance.** The headline earns attention by signalling who or what the message is for.
5. **Make a proposition.** Good copy gives the reader a reason to care now.
6. **Prefer proof over praise.** Facts, demonstrations, guarantees, testimonials and specific outcomes are stronger than self-congratulation.
7. **Allow personality.** Memorable wording can be playful, urgent, blunt, local, elegant or curious; it should not flatten every brand into the same polished tone.
8. **Use ordinary language.** The copy should sound speakable. If a real employee, customer or business owner would never say it, rewrite it.
9. **Keep hierarchy clear.** Historic print work often separates the arresting headline, explanatory support, offer and action.
10. **Do not confuse brevity with vagueness.** A short headline still needs a subject, tension, promise, fact or invitation.

## Human-copy research method

For each future research batch:

1. Select a defined archive, publication period or book chapter.
2. Capture the source reference and rights status.
3. Record the exact headline or a short compliant excerpt only when necessary.
4. Describe the communication pattern in original words.
5. Separate source-supported observation from modern interpretation.
6. Test the principle against modern social posts from different industries.
7. Record when the principle fails or becomes manipulative.
8. Create a Skill Card only when the pattern is reusable and evidence-backed.

## Anti-slop comparison method

For each proposed headline or caption, compare:

- **Generic version:** Could this line appear unchanged on hundreds of unrelated brands?
- **Specific version:** Does it contain the actual product, audience, moment, proof, offer, voice or tension?
- **Spoken test:** Would a real person naturally say this aloud?
- **Brand test:** Could the audience identify the client without seeing the logo?
- **Commercial test:** Does the line create a reason to stop, care, remember or act?
