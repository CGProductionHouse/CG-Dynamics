# Universal Marketing Brain Blueprint

Last updated: 2026-07-20
Status: Active foundation document
Scope: Cross-industry marketing knowledge used by every platform expert, industry expert and client brain

## 1. Purpose

The Universal Marketing Brain is the shared strategic foundation for the entire CG Dynamics AI marketing workforce.

It must teach the system how a strong human marketer thinks before it teaches the system how to write a caption, choose a format or imitate a platform trend.

It is not a folder of prompts, slogans or generic marketing tips.

It is a governed body of verified principles, tested applications, human judgement rules and reusable Skill Cards.

## 2. Core hierarchy

Universal Marketing Brain
→ Platform Expert
→ Industry Expert
→ Client Brain
→ Current context
→ Campaign brief
→ Creative work
→ Performance evidence
→ Learning

The Universal Marketing Brain supplies durable principles.

Platform Experts supply current platform mechanics.

Industry Experts supply category behaviour, customer motives, commercial realities and cultural context.

Client Brains supply the individual brand's voice, offers, history, people, proof, restrictions and performance.

No lower layer may overwrite stronger verified knowledge without evidence.

## 3. Universal standards

Every output created from this brain must be:

- commercially useful;
- grounded in a real audience, product, situation or objective;
- specific enough that unrelated brands could not use it unchanged;
- human enough to sound spoken, written or conceived by a thoughtful person;
- honest about evidence and uncertainty;
- appropriate to the client's actual personality;
- clear about whether a principle is durable, contextual, experimental or time-sensitive;
- designed to earn attention rather than merely fill a posting slot.

AI output is never a source.

## 4. Curriculum

### Domain 1 — Market and customer understanding

Questions this domain must answer:

- Who is the real buyer, user, influencer and blocker?
- What job is the person trying to get done?
- What functional, emotional, social and identity motives are present?
- What situation triggers the need?
- What language does the market naturally use?
- What objections, anxieties and trade-offs shape the decision?

Initial research themes:

- customer research;
- observation;
- interviews;
- jobs to be done;
- category entry points;
- awareness levels;
- problem sophistication;
- voice-of-customer evidence.

### Domain 2 — Positioning and differentiation

Questions this domain must answer:

- What does the brand want to be known for?
- Against what alternative is it being judged?
- What makes the promise believable?
- What can the brand credibly own?
- What should the brand deliberately not try to be?

Initial research themes:

- category and competitor frames;
- distinctiveness versus differentiation;
- brand memory structures;
- positioning statements;
- proof and reasons to believe;
- salience;
- brand assets.

### Domain 3 — Offer and commercial strategy

Questions this domain must answer:

- What is actually being sold?
- Why should someone act now?
- What risk, friction or uncertainty can be reduced?
- What value is created beyond discounting?
- Is the offer profitable and operationally deliverable?

Initial research themes:

- value propositions;
- offer construction;
- guarantees;
- trials and demonstrations;
- bundles;
- pricing presentation;
- urgency and scarcity;
- conversion friction;
- promotion fatigue.

### Domain 4 — Consumer psychology and behaviour

Questions this domain must answer:

- What attracts attention?
- What builds trust?
- What makes a message easier to understand and remember?
- What increases or reduces perceived risk?
- What social, emotional and cognitive forces influence action?

Initial research themes:

- social proof;
- authority;
- reciprocity;
- commitment and consistency;
- scarcity;
- loss aversion;
- processing fluency;
- salience;
- familiarity;
- emotion and memory;
- habit and ritual;
- identity signalling.

Psychology may never be used as a licence for manipulation or invented claims.

### Domain 5 — Human copy and language

Questions this domain must answer:

- What is worth saying?
- What would a real person actually say?
- What detail makes the message recognisable and ownable?
- What should be removed because it is empty, inflated or interchangeable?
- What rhythm, tone and structure best carry the idea?

Initial research themes:

- headlines;
- specificity;
- clarity;
- curiosity;
- conversational language;
- long and short copy;
- proof-led copy;
- calls to action;
- humour;
- voice;
- editing;
- anti-AI-slop detection.

Existing foundation assets include:

- Human Copy Over AI Slop;
- Cultural Observation Before Copy;
- Advertising Is Salesmanship;
- Specificity Over Superlatives;
- Offers Over Empty Claims.

### Domain 6 — Ideas, creativity and cultural observation

Questions this domain must answer:

- What human truth, tension or contradiction is present?
- What has the brand noticed that others have not expressed?
- Why is the idea timely?
- Is the brand entitled to say it?
- Is the execution merely clever, or does it advance the commercial objective?

Initial research themes:

- observation;
- creative strategy;
- lateral thinking;
- humour mechanics;
- participation;
- cultural context;
- topical work;
- brand entitlement;
- surprise;
- creative constraints.

### Domain 7 — Storytelling and message architecture

Questions this domain must answer:

- What should the audience understand first?
- What information earns the next second of attention?
- What tension, sequence or transformation carries the message?
- What belongs in the headline, visual, body and action?

Initial research themes:

- narrative tension;
- setup and payoff;
- problem, consequence and resolution;
- demonstration;
- case stories;
- testimonials;
- information hierarchy;
- hooks and openings;
- campaign systems.

### Domain 8 — Visual communication and art direction

Questions this domain must answer:

- Can the visual communicate before the caption is read?
- What should dominate the frame?
- Does the image contain product truth, human truth or proof?
- Is the work distinctive to the brand?
- Does the design improve comprehension or merely decorate the post?

Initial research themes:

- visual hierarchy;
- composition;
- typography;
- contrast;
- colour in context;
- product photography;
- people photography;
- demonstration;
- visual metaphor;
- brand consistency;
- poster and print-ad discipline;
- platform-safe adaptation.

### Domain 9 — Trust, proof and persuasion

Questions this domain must answer:

- Why should the audience believe this?
- What evidence is strongest for this specific decision?
- Is the proof current, relevant and representative?
- Are claims supportable?

Initial research themes:

- demonstrations;
- testimonials;
- reviews;
- credentials;
- guarantees;
- statistics;
- case studies;
- before and after evidence;
- process transparency;
- claim substantiation.

### Domain 10 — Campaign planning and channel roles

Questions this domain must answer:

- What is the campaign trying to change?
- What role does each channel play?
- What should be repeated and what should vary?
- How does one idea become a campaign rather than a single post?

Initial research themes:

- objectives;
- audience stages;
- message sequencing;
- reach and repetition;
- brand versus activation;
- paid, owned and earned roles;
- content systems;
- campaign calendars;
- frequency and fatigue.

### Domain 11 — Measurement, testing and learning

Questions this domain must answer:

- What outcome matters?
- What can be measured honestly?
- What hypothesis is being tested?
- What changed, and what cannot yet be concluded?
- What should be learned by the Universal Brain, Industry Expert or Client Brain?

Initial research themes:

- direct response tracking;
- experiments;
- test design;
- leading and lagging indicators;
- attribution limits;
- qualitative feedback;
- creative diagnostics;
- decision logs;
- learning confidence.

Existing foundation assets include Track Before Scaling.

### Domain 12 — Ethics, legality and brand safety

Questions this domain must answer:

- Is the claim truthful and supportable?
- Is the work exploiting a vulnerable audience?
- Is cultural humour respectful and contextually justified?
- Are privacy, consent, copyright and platform rules respected?
- Could the work damage trust even if it produces short-term engagement?

Initial research themes:

- truthful advertising;
- substantiation;
- privacy;
- consent;
- copyright;
- stereotyping;
- sensitive categories;
- children;
- health and financial claims;
- dark patterns;
- reputation risk.

## 5. Source strategy

### Tier 1 — Primary and authoritative

- original books and essays by recognised practitioners;
- peer-reviewed research;
- official regulatory material;
- official platform documentation;
- original campaign case studies with evidence;
- first-party business performance data.

### Tier 2 — Strong professional interpretation

- respected marketing institutes;
- established agency research;
- recognised industry publications;
- credible practitioner analysis;
- award case studies with sufficient strategic detail.

### Tier 3 — Discovery only

- general blogs;
- social posts;
- newsletters;
- opinion articles;
- unsourced summaries.

Tier 3 may identify a lead but may not activate a principle without stronger verification.

The Universal Marketing Brain will be built from the top down in this order:

2. Market and customer understanding.
3. Positioning and differentiation.
4. Offer and commercial strategy.
5. Consumer psychology and behaviour.
6. Human copy and language.
7. Ideas, creativity and cultural observation.
8. Storytelling and message architecture.
9. Visual communication and art direction.
10. Trust, proof and persuasion.
11. Campaign planning and channel roles.
12. Measurement, testing and learning.
13. Ethics, legality and brand safety.

Existing work must be mapped into this structure before new extraction begins.

## 8. Completion standard for each domain

A domain is implementation-ready only when it contains:

- a domain blueprint;
- a verified source inventory;
- source notes with traceable references;
- a set of atomic Skill Cards;
- misuse and limitation warnings;
- examples across more than one industry;
- an evaluation checklist;
- a review status;
- a learning and expiry policy where relevant.

No domain is complete merely because a long document exists.

## 9. Platform Expert relationship

Platform mechanics change frequently and must not be stored as timeless Universal Marketing principles.

Platform-specific knowledge must include:

- source URL or official document reference;
- date researched;
- last verified date;
- expected review frequency;
- expiry or stale status;
- country or account limitations;
- evidence level;
- change history.

Durable communication principles remain in the Universal Brain. Current distribution mechanics belong to Platform Experts.

## 10. Industry Expert priority policy

Industry Experts will be built according to the active-client industries in CG Dynamics, not an imaginary list of future sectors.

The app's active client records remain the source of truth for prioritisation.

The initial likely category groups, subject to confirmation against the active-client table, are:

- hospitality, restaurants, pubs and venues;
- fuel retail, convenience retail and multi-site operations;
- architecture, property and the built environment;
- events, entertainment and live experiences;
- professional and business services;
- connectivity, community services and local-market activation.

No inactive or former client should be embedded in the master Marketing Library.
