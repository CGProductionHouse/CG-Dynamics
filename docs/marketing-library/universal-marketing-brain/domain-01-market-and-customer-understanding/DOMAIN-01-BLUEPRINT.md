# Domain 01 — Market and Customer Understanding

Last updated: 2026-07-20
Status: Active research build
Scope: Durable methods for understanding real buyers, users, influencers, blockers, motives, triggers, language, anxieties and trade-offs

## Purpose

This domain teaches the AI workforce to understand people before producing strategy or creative work.

The system may not invent a customer profile from stereotypes, demographics or generic industry assumptions.

## Core principle

A market is not an abstract audience segment.

It is a group of people trying to make progress in specific situations while managing practical, emotional, social and identity trade-offs.

## Questions the domain must answer

- Who buys?
- Who uses?
- Who influences?
- Who approves or blocks?
- What happened immediately before the need appeared?
- What is the person trying to achieve or avoid?
- What alternative are they using now?
- What makes change feel risky or inconvenient?
- What words do they naturally use?
- What evidence is observed, what is reported and what is only assumed?

## Research evidence hierarchy

### Strongest evidence

- observed behaviour in the real environment
- recorded customer interviews about recent specific events
- first-party search, sales, support, review and performance data
- repeated patterns across independent participants
- transaction and behavioural evidence

### Useful supporting evidence

- credible market research
- industry statistics
- social listening
- competitor reviews
- frontline staff observations
- survey data with known sampling limits

### Weak evidence

- internal opinions
- invented personas
- demographic stereotypes
- generic AI summaries
- a single memorable anecdote
- what respondents say they would hypothetically do

Weak evidence may create a research question but may not activate a confident marketing principle.

## Core modules

### 1. Decision-role mapping

Separate:
- buyer
- end user
- influencer
- approver
- blocker
- recommender
- payer

One person may hold several roles. B2B and family purchases often involve several people.

### 2. Trigger and occasion mapping

Identify:
- the event or situation that creates demand
- the emotional and practical state at that moment
- urgency
- current workaround
- constraints
- place, time and company

Marketing should connect to real entry situations, not only customer traits.

### 3. Progress and jobs

Describe the progress the person is trying to make.

Use four lenses:
- functional
- emotional
- social
- identity

Do not reduce the job to the product category.

### 4. Current alternatives

The competitor is not always another brand.

Alternatives may include:
- doing nothing
- delaying
- handling it manually
- asking a friend
- using an existing supplier
- choosing a cheaper substitute
- avoiding the situation entirely

### 5. Forces affecting change

Map:
- dissatisfaction with the current situation
- attraction to the new solution
- anxiety about changing
- habit and comfort with the current behaviour

Do not overstate one force while ignoring the others.

### 6. Voice of customer

Capture exact language from:
- interviews
- sales calls
- reviews
- support messages
- search queries
- comments
- emails
- WhatsApp messages where consent and privacy allow

Store the original phrase, context, source, date and interpretation separately.

### 7. Objections, anxieties and trade-offs

Distinguish:
- price objection
- value uncertainty
- trust concern
- complexity
- timing
- social risk
- identity risk
- effort
- switching cost
- operational constraint

Do not treat every objection as a request for a discount.

### 8. Observation

Watch what people do in context.

Record:
- task
- tools
- workarounds
- interruptions
- sequence
- barriers
- other people involved
- environment
- visible emotion

Separate observation from interpretation.

### 9. Interview discipline

Ask about recent real behaviour.

Prefer:
- “Tell me about the last time…”
- “What happened next?”
- “What were you using before?”
- “What nearly stopped you?”
- “Who else was involved?”

Avoid:
- leading questions
- selling during research
- asking only what people like
- asking respondents to predict distant hypothetical behaviour

### 10. Pattern confidence

Every insight must record:
- evidence type
- sample
- recurrence
- segment or context
- contradictions
- confidence
- expiry or review need

## Minimum output for a client or industry study

- decision-role map
- trigger and occasion map
- progress statement
- current alternatives
- functional, emotional, social and identity motives
- anxieties and objections
- exact customer language
- evidence references
- contradictions and unknowns
- research questions still open

## Misuse warnings

- Do not create fictional personas and present them as research.
- Do not infer motive from age, race, gender or income alone.
- Do not treat interview statements as proven behaviour.
- Do not generalise one client’s audience to an entire industry.
- Do not publish private customer language without consent or safe anonymisation.
- Do not turn every observed frustration into a content idea before confirming commercial relevance.
