# Skill Card: Human copy over generic AI language

**Card ID:** HUM-COPY-001  
**Status:** `needs_review`  
**Confidence:** Medium  
**Category:** Copywriting / Brand Voice / Creative Review  
**Knowledge layer:** Universal marketing  
**Primary source note:** `source-notes/historical-advertising-and-human-copy.md`

## Principle

Write copy that sounds like a specific human speaking for a specific business in a specific moment.

Reject language that is polished but interchangeable, abstract or detached from the actual product, audience, place, offer, event, tension or proof.

## Why it matters

Generic AI copy creates sameness. It often uses vague verbs and empty transformation language because those phrases are broadly safe across industries. That safety removes personality, credibility and commercial force.

Human-sounding marketing is not casual wording for its own sake. It is communication grounded in recognisable situations, concrete details, brand character and a clear proposition.

## Source-supported foundation

Historical direct-response books and advertising archives repeatedly show communication built around:

- the actual product or event;
- a relevant audience or situation;
- concrete facts and specifics;
- a clear reason to care;
- proof or demonstration;
- an offer or action;
- distinct personality.

This card translates those patterns into a modern AI-writing control. It does not claim that historic advertisers used the term “AI slop.”

## Mandatory generation rules

Before approving any headline, poster copy, caption or script:

1. Name the real subject early.
2. Use concrete nouns and verbs.
3. Include at least one client-specific or situation-specific element when appropriate.
4. Preserve the client’s natural vocabulary, rhythm and level of formality.
5. Make the message speakable aloud.
6. Give the audience a reason to care, remember or act.
7. Prefer proof, detail or invitation over self-praise.
8. Remove words that could be pasted unchanged onto an unrelated brand.
9. Never add excitement through empty adjectives alone.
10. Do not invent slang, personality or local references the client would not use.

## Automatic rejection patterns

Flag for rewrite when copy relies on phrases such as:

- enhance your look;
- elevate your experience;
- unlock your potential;
- discover the difference;
- where quality meets excellence;
- redefine your style;
- take it to the next level;
- crafted to perfection;
- designed with you in mind;
- experience excellence;
- your journey starts here;
- make memories that last;
- more than just a [product];
- whether you are [A] or [B], we have you covered;
- step into a world of;
- indulge in;
- unleash;
- transform your;
- game-changing;
- seamless solutions.

These phrases are not forbidden in every conceivable context. They are presumed weak unless the surrounding copy makes them specific, credible and unmistakably on-brand.

## Interchangeability test

Replace the client name with three unrelated businesses.

If the line still works without meaningful changes, it is probably generic.

Example:

**Weak:** Enhance your look with confidence.

This could belong to a salon, eyewear store, clothing shop, dentist or cosmetics brand.

**Better directions:**

- Name the service or product.
- Name the occasion or customer problem.
- Use the client’s real vocabulary.
- Add a specific result, detail, offer or point of view.

## Speakable test

Read the line aloud and ask:

- Would the owner say this to a customer?
- Would a customer repeat it to a friend?
- Does it sound like something written for a poster rather than something generated to fill a poster?
- Is the sentence natural in the client’s language and market?

If not, rewrite.

## Specificity ladder

Move the copy down this ladder until it becomes real:

1. Abstract aspiration.
2. General benefit.
3. Named product or service.
4. Audience or situation.
5. Concrete detail or proof.
6. Distinct client voice.
7. Clear proposition or action.

Not every message needs all seven levels, but it should rarely stop at levels one or two.

## Headline construction options

Use one or more of these structures where appropriate:

- Product + real benefit.
- Audience + relevant situation.
- Problem + direct answer.
- Event + reason to attend.
- Offer + deadline or availability.
- Specific fact + implication.
- Question a customer genuinely asks.
- Strong opinion the brand can defend.
- Local moment + client relevance.
- Demonstration or proof.
- Familiar phrase rewritten in the client’s voice.

## Caption rules

A good caption should not merely repeat the poster.

It may:

- add the human context;
- explain why the offer matters;
- tell the short story behind the content;
- give practical details;
- invite a natural response;
- use the client’s real conversational style;
- include a clear next action.

Avoid padded introductions, generic motivational closings and unnecessary summaries.

## Brand inheritance

This universal Skill Card must be filtered through:

1. Industry Brain.
2. Client Brain.
3. Current campaign or Content Guide.
4. Platform and format.
5. South African and local context where relevant.

The universal rule removes generic language. The Client Brain determines what “human” sounds like for that specific business.

## Creative review output

When rejecting copy, do not merely say “sounds AI-generated.” Return:

- the generic phrase;
- why it is interchangeable;
- what client-specific information is missing;
- one stronger direction;
- two or three rewritten options in distinct tones.

## Example transformations

### Beauty

**Weak:** Enhance your look and embrace confidence.

**Direction:** Name the actual service, the customer moment and the visible result.

### Hospitality

**Weak:** Indulge in an unforgettable dining experience.

**Direction:** Name the dish, occasion, atmosphere, price, event or reason people are coming today.

### Entertainment

**Weak:** Make memories that last.

**Direction:** Name what is happening, who should come and what makes this night different.

### Professional services

**Weak:** Unlock your business potential with seamless solutions.

**Direction:** State the actual problem solved, process, evidence or outcome.

## Failure modes

- Becoming artificially quirky to avoid sounding generic.
- Using slang that does not belong to the client.
- Forcing jokes into serious brands.
- Mistaking short copy for good copy.
- Removing all polish and producing careless writing.
- Copying old advertisements instead of extracting principles.
- Using specificity that is invented or unsupported.
- Treating every client as informal.

## Human review checklist

- Is the line unmistakably relevant to the client?
- Does it name something real?
- Could a human naturally say it?
- Is there a clear reason to care?
- Is any claim supported?
- Does the tone fit the client?
- Does it avoid interchangeable AI phrasing?
- Would the line still make sense without decorative design?
