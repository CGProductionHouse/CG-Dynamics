# Skill Card: Cultural observation before copy

**Card ID:** HUMAN-COPY-002  
**Status:** `needs_review`  
**Confidence:** Medium  
**Knowledge layer:** Universal marketing craft  
**Primary use:** Brand strategy, campaign concepts, headlines, captions, poster copy, topical advertising, Content Guides  
**Review requirement:** Human creative review before activation

## Principle

Strong marketing often begins by noticing something people already do, feel, say, fear, celebrate or recognise.

Weak marketing often begins with a request to “make a post” and then fills the space with adjectives.

The agent should therefore search for the real human or cultural observation before it starts writing copy.

## Why this matters

Generic AI copy tends to begin at the sentence level:

- What headline can I generate?
- What adjective sounds premium?
- What slogan sounds polished?

Strong human creative work usually begins earlier:

- What is actually happening?
- What do people call this in real life?
- What tension, ritual, joke, frustration or desire already exists?
- Why is this brand entitled to speak about it?
- What can the audience participate in, recognise or repeat?

The line is the final expression of the thinking, not a substitute for the thinking.

## Evidence base

This draft card is informed by:

- Claude C. Hopkins' public-domain work on salesmanship, individuality, specificity and customer response.
- Coca-Cola's official account of Share a Coke, where a simple behaviour—sharing a named bottle—turned the product into a social action.
- Coca-Cola's official history of Boys on a Bench, where the cultural meaning of the image carried the communication.
- Nando's South Africa's official brand material, which grounds its identity in Southern African visual culture, local personality and a recognisable point of view.
- Historical newspaper, poster and magazine advertising archives that show direct subject-led headlines and propositions.

This card does not claim that every historical or famous campaign was effective. It extracts a recurring creative discipline for further human verification.

## Required thinking sequence

Before producing a concept or copy, answer:

1. **Business reality**  
   What is being sold, promoted, explained or changed?

2. **Audience reality**  
   What is the audience doing, saying, feeling or avoiding?

3. **Context**  
   Why does this matter now, in this place, for this client?

4. **Brand entitlement**  
   Why is this particular brand allowed to say this?

5. **Observation**  
   What recognisable human truth connects the first four answers?

6. **Creative decision**  
   What point of view will the work take?

7. **Expression**  
   What is the shortest clear line, image, action or format that communicates the idea?

Do not begin with the expression step.

## Cultural relevance rules

### Culture is context, not decoration

Do not add slang, memes, public holidays or trending audio simply to appear relevant.

A cultural reference is useful only when:

- It changes or strengthens the idea.
- The audience will recognise it naturally.
- The client has a credible reason to participate.
- The timing still makes sense.
- It does not exploit a serious issue carelessly.

### Local language must be earned

Do not insert South African slang mechanically.

Use local phrasing when it is:

- Natural for the client.
- Natural for the intended audience.
- Correct in meaning and tone.
- Consistent with how real people speak.

When uncertain, ask for human review rather than inventing authenticity.

### Topical work needs speed and judgement

A topical idea must pass:

- Relevance test.
- Timing test.
- Brand-entitlement test.
- Taste and risk test.
- Product connection test.

A clever comment with no connection to the business is not automatically useful marketing.

## Humour rules

Humour should emerge from observation, tension, character or timing.

Do not generate humour by:

- Adding random puns.
- Forcing slang.
- Making every brand sarcastic.
- Copying Nando's tone.
- Mocking customers.
- Turning a serious client into a meme account.

Humour must still do at least one useful job:

- Make the proposition easier to remember.
- Express the brand character.
- Reduce distance between brand and audience.
- Make a familiar truth newly visible.
- Encourage participation or sharing.

## Participation over description

Where possible, turn the audience from a reader into a participant.

Possible participation mechanisms:

- Choose.
- Vote.
- Name.
- Share.
- Complete a phrase.
- Tag the right person.
- Bring something.
- Try something.
- Attend.
- Respond with a real story.
- Recognise themselves in the idea.

Do not add engagement bait that has no relationship to the product or idea.

## Anti-slop checks

Reject or rewrite when the concept depends on:

- “Elevate”.
- “Enhance”.
- “Unlock”.
- “Experience the difference”.
- “Where quality meets...”.
- “More than just...”.
- “Your journey starts here”.
- “Designed with you in mind”.
- “Step into...”.
- “Discover a world of...”.

These phrases are not banned in every possible sentence. They are warning signals that the actual observation, product fact or proposition may be missing.

## Evaluation tests

### Observation test

Can the team state the real human observation in one sentence without using campaign language?

### Brand-right test

Could this exact idea come from three unrelated brands?

If yes, it needs more client truth.

### Product-connection test

Would the idea still make sense if the product, service or venue disappeared?

If yes, it may be entertainment rather than marketing.

### Speakability test

Would a real customer, employee or owner say the line aloud without sounding like a brochure?

### Care test

Does the work feel like someone noticed, chose and edited—or like the first acceptable sentence was used?

### Repeatability test

Is there a clear phrase, image, action or thought people can remember and repeat?

## Example transformation

### Weak starting point

> Elevate your dining experience with unforgettable flavours.

Why it fails:

- No actual dining situation.
- No product detail.
- No audience truth.
- No brand character.
- Could advertise almost any restaurant.

### Observation-led directions

These are directions, not final universal copy:

- The table orders chips “for everyone” and one person eats most of them.
- The friend who says they are not hungry always wants a bite.
- A match-day table has its own rituals and superstitions.
- Regulars know which sauce they can actually handle.
- The first cold drink after work has a different job from the second.

A client-specific agent would choose only observations that are true for that venue and then connect them to a real offer, event, dish or moment.

## Application to Content Guides

Before suggesting a video or poster, the agent should store:

- Observation.
- Business objective.
- Audience.
- Why now.
- Why this client.
- Proposed execution.
- Product or offer connection.
- Risk or sensitivity note.
- Evidence source or client insight.

The client should see the idea in simple language, not the internal strategy worksheet.

## Relationship to Client Brains

The Client Brain should accumulate approved observations such as:

- Real customer rituals.
- Staff personalities.
- Phrases customers use.
- Local references that worked.
- Humour boundaries.
- Topics the client rejected.
- Situations that repeatedly produce good content.
- Brand behaviours that feel authentic.

Do not convert one successful joke into a permanent tone rule without enough evidence.

## Misuse warnings

- Do not imitate famous campaigns directly.
- Do not make every brand edgy.
- Do not confuse controversy with cultural relevance.
- Do not manufacture a social position for a client that has not approved it.
- Do not use a public holiday merely because it appears on a calendar.
- Do not assume global internet culture matches a South African local audience.
- Do not allow “humanising” to become intentionally messy, unprofessional or inaccurate.

## Activation requirements

Before this card becomes Active:

- Review at least 30 South African campaigns.
- Review at least 30 strong international pre-2023 campaigns.
- Label the observation, business objective and brand entitlement in each.
- Test the framework against at least three active client categories.
- Record false positives where simple polished copy is human and effective.
- Complete human creative-director review.

## Current summary

The agent must not ask:

> What clever sentence can I generate?

It should ask:

> What did a thoughtful marketer notice here that is worth saying?
