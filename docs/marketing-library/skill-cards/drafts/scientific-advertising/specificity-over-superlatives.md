## Principle

Specific, concrete claims outperform vague superlatives. Telling a prospect exactly what you offer and what it does builds more trust than saying it is the best, greatest or most innovative.

## Summary

Hopkins argued that superlatives like best, greatest, amazing and revolutionary are meaningless to a reader. They create skepticism, not trust. A specific statement, such as a measurable benefit, a factual difference or a concrete result, gives the reader a real reason to believe and act. Specificity signals honesty. Superlatives signal exaggeration.

## Why it matters

Most advertising noise comes from vague claims that say nothing. Replacing them with specific facts immediately differentiates the message. This principle is especially important in a crowded feed where readers have learned to ignore generic praise.

## How to apply it

1. Replace every superlative with a specific, verifiable claim.
2. If the product saves time, say how much time. If it saves money, say how much money. If it improves results, say by what measure.
3. Avoid words like best, leading, innovative, revolutionising, game changing, next level, ultimate and similar empty descriptors.
4. Use numbers, timeframes, comparisons and factual differentiators instead.
5. Read every claim back and ask: would a sceptical prospect believe this?

## Examples

- Instead of best customer service, say we respond within two hours.
- Instead of high quality ingredients, say made with free range eggs and stone ground flour.
- Instead of innovative software, say reduces data entry time by 40 percent.
- Instead of trusted by leading brands, say used by 15 South African real estate agencies.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Adding specific numbers that cannot be verified. False specificity destroys trust faster than vague claims.
- Using vague specificity such as over 10 years experience when 10 years is the minimum possible claim.
- Believing that specificity alone is enough. The specific claim must also be relevant to what the customer cares about.
- Overloading every sentence with numbers. One strong specific claim is better than a paragraph of weak data.
