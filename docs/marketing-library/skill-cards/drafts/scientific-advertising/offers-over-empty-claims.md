## Principle

The offer is the most important part of any advertisement. A clear, relevant offer will outperform a beautiful ad with no real reason to act.

## Summary

Hopkins centred advertising on the idea that people respond to offers, not to advertising. The advertisement is just the delivery mechanism for the offer. If the offer is weak, the advertising cannot save it. If the offer is strong, the advertising only needs to communicate it clearly. Price alone is rarely the best offer. Service, convenience, risk reduction, guarantee, exclusivity and specific results all outperform a simple price reduction.

## Why it matters

This card prevents marketing teams from blaming creative execution when the real problem is a weak offer. It forces the question: what are we actually giving the customer, and why should they care? Without a strong offer, no amount of good copy or design will produce consistent results.

## How to apply it

1. Before writing any ad, state the offer in one sentence.
2. If the offer is a discount, ask whether there is a stronger non-price offer available.
3. Lead with the offer, not with branding. The customer needs to know what they get before they care who it is from.
4. Make the offer specific enough that the customer can imagine using it.
5. Reduce risk in the offer where possible. Guarantees, free trials and consultations remove hesitation.

## Examples

- Instead of we are the leading provider of X, lead with get your first consultation free and a written quote within 24 hours.
- Instead of a seasonal sale, lead with book by Friday and receive a free strategy session.
- Instead of browse our range, lead with answer three questions and we will send you a personalised product recommendation.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Leading with brand positioning when the customer has not yet decided to engage.
- Treating a discount as the only possible offer.
- Making an offer that sounds good but has no real value to the customer.
- Hiding the offer behind long copy or design flourishes. The offer should be visible immediately.
- Using an offer that cannot be delivered. A strong offer that fails in delivery destroys trust faster than a weak offer.
