## Principle

Advertising exists to serve the customer, not to win awards, impress peers or stroke the creative ego.

## Summary

Hopkins warned against advertising that exists to show off rather than to sell. He argued that the temptation to make clever, artistic or award-worthy advertising is a distraction from the real purpose: persuading someone to take action. The customer does not care how creative the ad is. They care about whether it speaks to their need and offers a solution. Advertising should be judged by results, not by applause.

## Why it matters

This card is a corrective force. It keeps marketing grounded in business outcomes. It prevents teams from spending client money on concepts that exist to impress other marketers rather than to reach real customers. It also protects the agency from the trap of doing portfolio work instead of effective work.

## How to apply it

1. Judge every piece of advertising by the question: does this help the customer make a decision?
2. Remove any element that exists to impress rather than to persuade.
3. Be willing to run simple, direct advertising that works over clever advertising that is noticed but not acted on.
4. When presenting creative work, lead with the strategy and the expected outcome, not with the creative concept.
5. Celebrate results, not awards. Internal recognition should go to campaigns that moved metrics, not to campaigns that looked good in a meeting.

## Examples

- Choosing a clear, direct headline that states the offer over a clever, ambiguous headline that requires interpretation.
- Running a simple before and after comparison instead of a cinematic brand film that does not communicate a specific benefit.
- Writing a caption that explains exactly what the reader will get instead of a cryptic one-liner that sounds profound but says nothing.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Confusing creativity with effectiveness. An ad can be both creative and effective, but creativity without persuasion is a waste.
- Using client budgets to build an agency portfolio.
- Pitching advertising that the creative team loves but that does not pass the does this help the customer test.
- Assuming that award-winning advertising is the benchmark. Most award-winning advertising is not profitable advertising.
- Allowing creative teams to override customer research because the less effective option is more interesting to produce.

## Agent instructions

- Use this card when reviewing any piece of copy or creative work to check whether it serves the customer or the creator.
- Use this card when generating campaign concepts to bias toward clarity and persuasion over cleverness.
- Use this card when writing client reports to frame success around customer response rather than creative recognition.
- Do not use this card to dismiss all creative ambition. The principle is about priority, not about banning creativity.
