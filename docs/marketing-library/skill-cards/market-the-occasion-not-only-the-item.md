# Skill Card: Market the occasion, not only the item

Card ID: HOSP-001
Status: needs_review
Domain: Hospitality
Category: Strategy and content planning
Confidence: Medium
Extrapolation risk: Medium
Human review required: Yes

## Principle

Hospitality marketing becomes more useful when it connects the product or venue to a real human occasion instead of merely displaying an item and praising it.

## Why it matters

A customer rarely chooses food or a venue in a vacuum. The decision is shaped by context such as time, company, energy, budget, weather, routine, celebration, convenience or social purpose.

An item-only post says what exists.

An occasion-led post helps the customer recognise when and why it fits their life.

## Required thinking sequence

Before creating content, answer:

1. What is the commercial objective?
2. What real occasion can create that behaviour?
3. What is the customer doing or feeling immediately before the decision?
4. What friction or desire exists?
5. What product, service or venue truth answers it?
6. What proof can we show?
7. When should the content reach them?
8. What action should follow?

## Occasion examples

- Breakfast before work
- Coffee after school drop-off
- Quick lunch between meetings
- Friday after-work drink
- Date night
- Family dinner
- Saturday sport
- Sunday lunch
- Birthday
- Payday treat
- Month-end value meal
- Rainy-day comfort
- Hot-weather refreshment
- No-energy takeaway night
- Travelling through town
- Hosting friends at home

## Weak pattern

Product photo + generic adjective + broad invitation.

Example:
"Indulge in our mouth-watering burger and elevate your dining experience."

Why it fails:
- no identifiable person or situation
- no reason to choose it now
- no concrete product truth
- interchangeable with thousands of businesses
- sounds written to fill a caption slot

## Stronger direction

Start from the situation.

Example observation:
Everyone says they are ordering their own chips until the first plate reaches the table.

Possible content direction:
Show the shared-table behaviour, make the chips central to the social tension, and attach the real ordering or booking action.

The exact line must come from the client’s voice and current offer. Do not reuse the observation mechanically across brands.

## Output requirements

A proposed hospitality post should include:

- Objective
- Occasion
- Audience state
- Product or experience truth
- Human observation
- Proof
- Format
- Timing
- Call to action
- Operational check

## Guardrails

- Do not invent customer behaviour and present it as fact.
- Do not force every item into a joke.
- Do not ignore straightforward functional occasions such as speed, price or convenience.
- Do not exploit sensitive cultural or religious occasions.
- Do not create an offer the venue cannot operationally deliver.
- Do not confuse a topical date with a relevant occasion.
- Do not erase the actual product while chasing relatability.

## Decision test

Ask:

- Would a real customer recognise this situation?
- Is this business genuinely relevant to it?
- Does the product solve, support or improve the occasion?
- Is there a reason to see the message at this time?
- Could an unrelated venue use the content unchanged?

If the final answer is yes, the work is still too generic.
